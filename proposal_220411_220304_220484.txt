Abstract
Grading short-answer questions in education is time-consuming, often inconsistent, and strongly influenced
by subjective teacher judgment. As a result, students receive feedback late and evaluation may become less
fair. This project focuses on using Natural Language Processing (NLP) to automatically understand student
answers, identify key concepts, and assign rubric-based scores. The system will be built using supervised
NLP models and will follow a human-in-the-loop approach, where teachers can review, approve, and correct
AI-generated scores and feedback before final grading. Teacher corrections will be stored as high-quality
annotations and used to improve the NLP model over time. In addition to scoring, the system will generate
short, clear, and useful feedback that helps students understand mistakes and improve learning outcomes. The
platform will also provide learning analytics dashboards that summarize class performance, highlight common
misconceptions, and support data-driven teaching decisions. The research will involve building a
teacher-annotated dataset, training and evaluating the NLP models using reliability and error metrics, and
testing the system with educators. The expected outcome is an intelligent web-based platform that reduces
grading workload, improves feedback speed and consistency, and provides meaningful analytics for classroom
improvement.
Keywords — automated short-answer grading, NLP, supervised learning, human-in-the-loop, teacher-guided
AI, formative feedback, learning analytics.

1.​Introduction
Assessment is important in education because it helps teachers understand what students have learned and
where they need support. However, grading short-answer questions manually takes a lot of time, especially in
large classes. It can also be inconsistent, because different teachers may give different scores to similar
answers, and students often receive feedback too late.
In recent years, Natural Language Processing (NLP) and machine learning have shown strong potential for
improving short-answer grading. Modern NLP models, including transformer-based approaches, can better
understand meaning in text and can be used to automatically score student answers. However, scoring alone
is not enough, because students also need clear feedback to learn from mistakes. In addition, learning
analytics can help teachers track progress, identify common misconceptions, and make better teaching
decisions. This research aims to develop an intelligent web platform that combines automated assessment,
feedback generation, and learning analytics for short answers. The platform will use NLP models together
with a teacher-guided AI workflow, where teachers can review and correct AI outputs. This approach is
expected to improve accuracy, support teacher trust, and continuously improve the system using real
classroom data.

2.​Problem Statement
Short-answer questions are widely used in education because they help measure real understanding. However,
grading short answers manually takes a lot of time, especially in large classes. As a result, students often
receive feedback late, which reduces its value for learning. This problem mainly affects teachers, who face a
heavy workload, and students, who need faster support to improve.
Manual grading can also be inconsistent. Because short answers are open-ended, similar answers may receive
different scores depending on the teacher. This reduces fairness and transparency. Although Automated Short

Answer Grading (ASAG) systems exist, many of them focus only on predicting a score and do not provide
clear feedback that helps students learn. In addition, many NLP-based methods require high-quality
teacher-annotated data, which is difficult to collect in real classrooms. Finally, most solutions do not include
learning analytics dashboards that help teachers track progress and identify common mistakes. Therefore, there
is a need for a practical platform that combines NLP-based scoring, meaningful feedback, teacher review, and
learning analytics in one system.

4. Novelty
The novelty of this project lies in combining several important components into one integrated and
teacher-centered platform. While many existing Automated Short Answer Grading (ASAG) systems
focus mainly on predicting scores, this work aims to provide a complete workflow that includes
scoring, feedback, teacher review, and learning analytics in a single web-based system. In particular,
the platform will use modern NLP models to understand the meaning of student answers and
generate rubric-based scores, while also producing short and useful feedback. Another novel aspect
is the teacher-guided (human-in-the-loop) design, where teachers can approve and correct
AI-generated outputs before final grading. These corrections will be stored as high-quality
annotations and used to continuously improve the NLP models over time. In addition, the system will

provide learning analytics dashboards that help teachers identify common misconceptions and
monitor student progress. This integrated approach is expected to improve accuracy, increase
teacher trust, and make the system more practical for real classroom use.

6.​ Literature Review
Many studies show that grading short answers manually takes a lot of time and can be inconsistent.
Because of this, researchers have developed Automated Short Answer Grading (ASAG) systems using
NLP and machine learning. Modern deep learning and transformer models can understand meaning better
than old keyword-based methods, so they achieve better scoring results.
However, scoring alone is not enough. Students also need feedback to learn from mistakes, but automatic
feedback generation is still difficult. Another important area is learning analytics, which helps teachers see
student progress, common errors, and performance trends.
Recent research also shows that teachers trust AI systems more when they can review and correct the results.
This is why teacher-guided AI (human-in-the-loop) is important. It improves accuracy and makes the
system more acceptable for real classrooms.
Overall, the literature supports automated grading, but highlights the need to combine scoring, feedback,
analytics, and teacher involvement in one platform.

7.​ Research Design and Methodology
a.​ Research Design
This study adopts a mixed-method research design, combining quantitative experimental evaluation of
the AI-based grading model with qualitative teacher feedback to assess usability, trust, and adoption. The
research follows a prototype development and validation approach, where an intelligent web platform is
designed and implemented to support automated assessment, feedback generation, and learning analytics
for short-answer responses. The system will be developed using NLP techniques and evaluated against
teacher-generated baseline scores and feedback.

b.​ Mapping Research Questions to Methods
To ensure alignment between the research questions and the proposed methodology, each research question
will be addressed using specific methods:
RQ1 (Platform and AI model development): Addressed through system design and
prototype implementation, including automated scoring and feedback generation using
supervised NLP.
RQ2 (AI vs. teacher evaluation): Addressed through experimental testing and
statistical comparison between AI-generated scores and teacher scores using agreement
and error metrics.
RQ3 (Learning analytics integration): Addressed through the design and
implementation of analytics dashboards, followed by evaluation of their usefulness for
teachers.
➔​ Student answers → NLP scoring → feedback → teacher review → store corrections → analytics

c.​ Data Collection and Analysis
The dataset will be collected through collaboration with teachers, who will provide real short-answer
responses from students. Teachers will grade these answers using a clear scoring rubric and, where possible,
provide short feedback aligned with learning objectives. All student responses will be anonymized before
processing to ensure privacy and ethical compliance.
For quantitative evaluation, the AI model will be trained using supervised learning and tested on unseen
responses. The predicted scores will be compared with teacher scores using reliability and accuracy metrics
such as Mean Absolute Error (MAE), correlation, and inter-rater agreement measures (e.g., Cohen’s kappa
or ICC). In addition, grading speed will be measured to evaluate time savings compared to manual grading.
For qualitative evaluation, teacher surveys or interviews will be conducted to assess usability, trust,
transparency, and the perceived usefulness of AI-generated feedback and learning analytics dashboards.
Qualitative responses will be analyzed using thematic analysis to identify key adoption factors and
improvement needs.

8. Data Availability for Experiments
The experiments in this project will be conducted using a dataset of real short-answer responses collected
through collaboration with teachers. The dataset will include student answers, teacher-assigned scores based on
a clear scoring rubric, and where possible, short teacher feedback aligned with learning objectives. All student
responses will be anonymized by removing personal identifiers to ensure privacy and ethical compliance. The
expected dataset size is approximately 1,000–3,000 short-answer responses in the initial stage, and it may be
expanded over time through teacher corrections during system use. The data will be stored in a structured
format such as CSV or JSON, where each record includes the question, student answer, teacher score, and
optional teacher feedback. If real classroom data is limited at the early stage, a small synthetic dataset may be
created for initial testing of the platform workflow, but the main evaluation will be based on teacher-annotated
answers. The dataset will be used only for research and evaluation purposes, and access will be restricted to the
project team unless explicit permission is granted for future academic sharing.

9. Experimental Setup (Initial Plan)
The system will be evaluated using short-answer responses that were not used during training. The main goal is
to check how close the AI scores are to teacher scores. The evaluation will use metrics such as Mean Absolute
Error (MAE), correlation, and agreement measures (e.g., Cohen’s kappa or ICC). We will also measure grading
time to estimate how much faster the platform is compared to manual grading. To make the results meaningful,
the proposed transformer-based NLP model will be compared with simple baseline methods, such as TF-IDF
similarity and sentence embedding similarity (e.g., SBERT). The experiments will be implemented in Python
using Hugging Face Transformers and PyTorch or TensorFlow. If needed, GPU resources will be used to speed
up model training.

10. Ablation Study
An ablation study will be used to understand which parts of the system are most important. We will test the
model with different configurations and compare the results. For example, we will evaluate scoring
performance with and without teacher correction data to measure the impact of the human-in-the-loop
workflow. We will also compare a transformer-based model with simpler NLP baselines, such as TF-IDF or
sentence embeddings. This will help identify which components contribute most to accuracy and reliability.

12.​Ethical Considerations and Data Management
a.​ Research Ethics and Participant Protection
This research involves the collection of short-answer responses graded by teachers and may include teacher
participation in usability testing. Before any data collection begins, written informed consent will be
obtained from participating teachers. Student responses used in the dataset will be treated as educational data
and will be anonymized by removing all personal identifiers. Participation will be voluntary, and teachers
will have the right to withdraw at any stage without any negative consequences.
If required by university policy, ethical clearance will be requested from the appropriate academic or
institutional review committee before collecting any classroom data.

b.​ Responsible Data Management
All collected data, including student responses, teacher scores, teacher feedback, and teacher corrections of
AI outputs, will be securely stored in password-protected and access-controlled storage. Only the research
team members will have access to the dataset. The dataset will be version-controlled to ensure transparency
and prevent unauthorized modifications.
Data will be used strictly for research purposes, model training, and evaluation. After the project is
completed, the dataset will either be archived securely for academic use or deleted, depending on university
requirements and participant consent.

c.​ Ethical Use of AI Tools
AI-assisted tools may be used only for language refinement, grammar checking, and clarity improvement
during proposal writing. These tools will not be used to fabricate results, generate false data, or replace
original research work. All outputs will be reviewed and edited manually to ensure academic integrity.

d.​ Academic Integrity Statement
This research will follow the university’s academic integrity policies. All external academic sources will be
cited properly. The project will avoid plagiarism, data fabrication, and unethical use of AI. Teacher and
student data will be handled responsibly, with respect for privacy and confidentiality.

13.​ Feasibility, Limitations, and Risk Management
This research is feasible because modern NLP tools and web technologies are widely available. The
platform can be developed using Python, transformer-based NLP libraries, and a standard web framework.
The project will be built as a prototype and improved step by step.
However, there are some limitations and risks. The main challenge is collecting enough high-quality
teacher-annotated short answers for training and evaluation. To reduce this risk, the project will start
with a smaller dataset and expand it gradually through teacher corrections.
Another limitation is that AI may not always grade complex or unclear answers correctly. To manage this,
the platform will use a teacher-guided AI approach, where teachers can review and correct AI outputs before
final grading.
Technical risks include limited GPU resources for training NLP models. This can be handled by using
university resources, cloud services, or smaller models. Finally, teacher adoption may be difficult if the
system is not trusted or easy to use, so the platform will focus on usability, transparency, and practical
analytics dashboards.

14.​ Expected Outcomes
This research is expected to produce a working prototype of an intelligent web platform for
automated assessment, feedback generation, and learning analytics of short answers. The platform
will be able to automatically score student responses and generate short, helpful feedback.
The system will also provide learning analytics dashboards for teachers, such as class performance
summaries, progress tracking, and common mistake detection. In addition, the project will produce
a teacher-annotated dataset of short answers, including scores and feedback, which can be used for
model training and evaluation.
The evaluation results are expected to show that the platform reduces grading time and improves consistency
compared to fully manual grading. Teacher usability testing is also expected to provide insights about trust,
ease of use, and real classroom adoption.
